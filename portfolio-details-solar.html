Overview

Can solar energy realistically meet Singapore's household demand?

This project investigates whether photovoltaic systems can achieve Singapore's Green Plan 2030 and net-zero 2050 targets, framed as a data science question rather than a policy assumption. Nine datasets spanning 2005–2025 were integrated across weather, consumption, and solar generation records to quantify the energy gap, identify what's actually driving consumption growth, and model when (or whether) break-even is achievable.

The answer: under current trends, solar meets just 8.3% of household demand in 2025, with break-even projected at 2144. Targeted interventions can move that to 2099.

Course project · SC3021 Data Science Fundamentals · with Andrea Nicho Aruldass & Renee Teo Rui Ning

Analytical Framework

Four layers of analysis, each answering a different question

1
Descriptive
What happened?
Time-series decomposition isolating trend, seasonal, and residual components. Identified +108 GWh/year linear growth, symmetrical May–July cooling peaks, and residual spikes at 2011 (El Niño) and 2021 (COVID-19 WFH).
2
Diagnostic
Why did it happen?
Fishbone diagrams and 5 Whys analysis to attribute consumption drivers: work-from-home adoption, appliance proliferation, urban heat island effect (+2–4°C), and variable building insulation. Events correlated against meteorological records.
3
Predictive
What will happen?
Linear regression on 70/30 chronological split (2005–2019 train, 2020–2025 test). RMSE: 132.24 GWh, error: 1.66%. 10-year forecast projects ~9,200 GWh by 2035. ARIMA rejected due to overfitting risk on a 21-year dataset.
4
Prescriptive
What should be done?
Scenario modelling for policy interventions: aggressive solar expansion (870 MW → 3,000 MW), time-of-use pricing, cool roof mandates, and household efficiency measures. Each modelled for impact on break-even year.
Key Engineering Contributions

Where the technical decisions were made

01
End-to-End Data Pipeline
Ingested 9 heterogeneous datasets from SP Group, EMA, NASA POWER, and Meteorological Service Singapore. A structured profiling checklist was applied before any transformation: type consistency, missing value thresholding (<5%), temporal overlap validation, IQR-based outlier detection, and format standardisation across date formats and energy units.
02
Physics-Informed Solar Output Model
Solar output estimated using: Capacity × Sunshine Hours × Performance Ratio × Days, with a conservative PR of 0.85 to account for Singapore's real-world efficiency losses of 10–15% below ideal irradiance. Quarterly capacity data was interpolated to monthly resolution before applying the formula.
03
Consumption Reconstruction Pipeline
National household consumption estimated as: Σ(Household Count × Avg Consumption × 12) across 6 standardised dwelling types, aggregated from district-level to national scope. A 1.095× scaling factor aligned outputs with official Statista benchmarks. Final validation: RMSE 132.24 GWh, percentage error 1.66%.
04
Regression Forecasting & Break-Even Modelling
70/30 chronological train/test split, not random, to simulate genuine out-of-sample forecasting. Coefficients: slope = 108.11 GWh/year. Break-even modelled by projecting solar growth (exponential curve fitted from 2008–2025 policy-driven expansion) against consumption trend. Policy scenarios modelled as additive offsets to the consumption trajectory.
05
Anomaly Attribution — COVID & El Niño
Time-series residuals were cross-referenced against meteorological records and historical event logs to distinguish structural from transient variance. The 2021 spike (+~300 GWh above trend) was attributed to COVID-19 WFH adoption; the 2011 residual to El Niño temperature extremes. Distinguishing these prevents the model from encoding noise as signal.
06
Weather-Energy Correlation Analysis
Pearson correlation between monthly temperature and per-account consumption: r = 0.073, p = 0.22 — statistically insignificant. The finding is substantively interesting: unlike temperate climates, Singapore's year-round warmth means AC usage is baseline, not temperature-responsive. This null result is a key insight, not a failure.
