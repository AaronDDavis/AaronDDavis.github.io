Overview

Allocating 6,000+ students when constraints fight each other

The problem is deceptively simple: split a cohort of students into balanced teams. The constraint is not. Each team must simultaneously approximate the tutorial-wide gender ratio, keep average CGPA within a tight band of the tutorial mean, and contain students from multiple schools. These constraints frequently conflict, optimising for one degrades another. Exhaustive enumeration is computationally impossible at this scale (~1015 configurations for 6-student teams from 50 students). The solution is a randomised iterative engine with adaptive feedback.

Course project · SC1003 Introduction to Computational Thinking and Programming

Constraint Definitions
Gender Balance
malesi = round((M ÷ (M+F)) × si)
Proportional allocation per team, mirroring tutorial-wide ratio.
Strict: adjusted only as last resort.
CGPA Uniformity
|avgteam − avgtutorial| ≤ ε
ε starts at 0.005, widens by 0.005 every 250K failed iterations.
Primary relaxation lever.
School Diversity
unique_schools ≥ d
d starts at team size, decreases when ε exceeds 0.015–0.025.
Secondary relaxation lever.
Key Engineering Contributions

Randomisation, validation, and knowing when to give up just enough

01
Randomised Iterative Allocation Engine
Candidate teams are generated by stochastically sampling students according to per-group gender targets. Each candidate is validated against CGPA and diversity thresholds before being committed; invalid teams are discarded and students returned to the pool for retry. This explores the solution space efficiently without a formal objective function or exhaustive enumeration.
02
Dynamic Constraint Relaxation
Three iteration counters (j, k, l) detect convergence failure at group, tutorial, and extreme levels respectively. When k fires, CGPA tolerance widens by 0.005. When ε exceeds 0.015–0.025, school diversity minimum decreases. Gender targets are adjusted only a last resort when l fires. The hierarchy reflects real constraint rigidity.
03
Guaranteed Termination
Constraints relax monotonically i.e. they never tighten mid-run. This guarantees the algorithm always terminates: in the limit, with ε → ∞ and d → 1, any allocation is valid. In practice, relaxation is minimal; the algorithm converges to the tightest feasible thresholds for the given tutorial's student composition, typically within seconds.
04
Proportional Gender Targeting
Gender targets are derived from the tutorial-wide M:F ratio applied proportionally to each team size: malesi = round((M / (M+F)) × si). Remainder students (from uneven divisions) are absorbed into a final team and validated separately. This ensures no team deviates systematically from the cohort gender distribution.
05
Optimal Remainder Distribution
Team size is user-specified (4–10), but cohort size is rarely a perfect multiple. Remainders are distributed by growing the last few teams by 1, rather than creating an undersized outlier. For example, 50 students at size 8 yields 4 teams of 8 and 2 teams of 9, all within the valid range.
06
Scale: 6K+ Students, Zero Dependencies
The entire implementation uses only Python's standard library — csv and random. No third-party packages. Tested on cohorts of 6K+ students across 120+ tutorial groups, typically converging in seconds. Worst-case tutorials (highly constrained compositions) may take 1–2 minutes, bounded by the relaxation schedule.
Execution Flow

How a run progresses

1
Parse records.csv → organise students by tutorial group
2
User inputs team size (4–10). Remainder distribution computed.
3
For each tutorial: separate by gender → compute proportional targets per team
4
Randomly sample → validate CGPA + diversity → commit or reject & retry. On repeated failure → relax constraints.
5
Output teams per tutorial group with student IDs, names, gender, school, CGPA
